Selected Digital Forensics Labs

Preparing a Water-Damaged Phone | Mobile Digital Forensics I

There will often be situations where an examiner will come across a water-damaged phone. In this lab, the phone is completely destroyed from 5 days of salt water. However, the board can still be prepared for a chip-off.

Hand Carving | Mobile Digital Forensics I

We did hand carving in the first digital forensics class. Though programs like Cellebrite can carve files out for you, it is important to understand the underlying techniques that these programs use. Plus, it's a lot of fun to manually carve things out and can be done with free tools.

Manually Constructing the IMSI | Mobile Digital Forensics I

The IMSI uniquely identifies users on cellular networks. This optional lab explored how IMSI's are created, and how one might use known information to construct the IMSI.

Cellebrite's Physical Analyzer | Mobile Digital Forensics II

Most of Mobile Digital Forensics II was spent working through a 10-chapter lab book focused solely on Cellebrite's Physical Analyzer. This particular lab was an exercise in using bookmarks in Cellebrite's Physical Analyzer to document potentially valuable information.

Validation Using UltraCompare | Mobile Digital Forensics II

In mobile digital forensics, we can't just block writing to the phone like we might with regular digital forensics. Therefore, some non-evidential changes might be made. This shows that, while some changes might be made, they will not affect the evidence. Validation is essential when it comes to court proceedings.

Manually Finding Timestamps | Mobile Digital Forensics II

We actually spent a good amount of time on timestamps. Timestamps on mobile devices can certainly be very confusing, and you are sometimes dealing with multiple kinds of timestamps on a single device. This is an example of one of the labs where we were looking into timestamps.

Thermal Chip-Off & Chip Cleaning Lab | Mobile Digital Forensics III

This lab consisted of 16 chip-offs. All of these chip-offs were done from home due to Covid19, so I'm proud that I was able to gain familiarity with the rework station all by myself. The 'after' image is what the chip looks like when it's ready to read. The 'before' image is what the chip looks like before it is cleaned.

Thermal Chip-Off, Chip Cleaning, & Chip Reading | Mobile Digital Forensics III

This was the lab portion of the final exam. I performed a thermal chip-off, cleaned the removed chip, and read it for contents successfully.

Chip Identification Lab | Mobile Digital Forensics III

This lab helped me gain familiarity with the different types of chips in mobile devices, what they do, and how to research them.

Performing a JTAG with a Molex Cable | Mobile Digital Forensics III

JTAG techniques can be useful on some older devices. This lab helped me gain familiarity with JTAG software.

Non-thermal Chip Removal | Mobile Digital Forensics III

Non-thermal chip-offs can be less risky in some cases. You can then use lap and polishing or milling to get the chip to a readable state.

Soldering a RIFF Jumper Board | Mobile Digital Forensics III

To save time for cases where a RIFF adapter / jumper board is needed for a JTAG, it can be helpful to construct your own. This lab was instrumental in teaching me how to solder.
